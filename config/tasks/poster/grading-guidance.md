# Poster Presentation Grading Guidance

Grade only the configured categories: Poster and Presentation.

Use only evidence from the uploaded poster and the conversation transcript. Do not infer that the student did something well unless the poster or conversation supports it.

## Evidence and review rule

Return one overall review status for the completed attempt:

- `Review (low confidence)` if evidence is limited, ambiguous, missing, inaccessible, or should be checked manually before use.
- `OK (medium confidence)` if the evidence is adequate for an advisory mark but some uncertainty remains.
- `OK (high confidence)` if the poster and transcript provide strong, direct evidence for the advisory marks.

## Required poster elements

The poster should include and integrate:

1. **Introduction to the network studied**
   Clearly identify the nodes, ties, and broader context of the network, and explain why the network is substantively interesting or important.

2. **Network visualization optional but encouraged**
   Include a network visualization if appropriate, and interpret visible structures or patterns such as clustering, communities, central actors, isolates, brokerage, homophily, or segregation.

3. **Descriptive network statistics with interpretation**
   Present at least one descriptive network statistic, for example density, clustering, centralization, reciprocity, modularity, degree distribution, or average path length, and explain what it reveals about the structure or behavior of the network.

4. **Theoretical framework and hypotheses**
   Clearly state hypotheses about network formation and/or network effects, and justify them using relevant theoretical reasoning or prior research.

5. **Results from one or two statistical network models**
   Present key findings from network formation and/or network effects models, including substantive interpretation of coefficients or parameter estimates.

6. **Concluding insights and implications**
   Summarize the main takeaway from the project and connect the findings back to the broader research question, substantive motivation, or real-world implication.

## Poster category

Evaluate the uploaded artifact itself. Strong posters should be clear, readable, well organized, and substantively integrated. They should not merely display outputs; they should help a viewer understand the research question, the network, the theory, the methods, the findings, and the implications.

**Exemplary 90-100:** The poster is well balanced, visually engaging, and clearly structured. All six required elements are present, distinct, and effectively integrated. Key findings are highlighted, and the poster connects theory, methods, empirical results, and conclusion. Concise summary statements are used effectively.

**Proficient 80-89:** The poster is clear and organized, with only minor issues in balance, design, section clarity, or integration. Most key elements are present. The connection across theory, methods, results, and conclusion may be present but not fully developed.

**Developing 70-79:** The poster is unevenly organized, overly cluttered, sparse, or missing important sections. It may describe outputs without enough interpretation, or it may lack coherence across theory, methods, findings, and implications.

**Unacceptable <70:** The poster is incomplete, disorganized, very difficult to read, or substantively inaccurate. Multiple required elements are missing or misrepresented.

## Presentation category

Evaluate the student's conversation responses as evidence of poster-session presentation quality. Strong presentations explain the poster clearly, respond directly to questions, interpret evidence rather than only naming it, and communicate the main takeaway.

**Exemplary 90-100:** The student explains the project clearly and confidently, adapts to questions, communicates key insights, interprets evidence accurately, and shows strong command of the network, theory, model, and conclusion.

**Proficient 80-89:** The student gives clear and generally prepared answers. They can explain the main project and respond to most questions, though some answers may lack depth, precision, or adaptability.

**Developing 70-79:** The student's explanations are uneven, overly scripted, vague, or incomplete. Responses to questions are limited, unclear, or only loosely connected to the poster.

**Unacceptable <70:** The student appears unprepared, cannot explain central parts of the poster, or is unable to respond meaningfully to questions.

## Relationship between poster and presentation

Score Poster and Presentation separately.

If the uploaded poster is strong but the student cannot explain it, keep the Poster score strong but lower the Presentation score.

If the student explains the project well but required material is missing from the uploaded poster, keep the Presentation score strong but lower the Poster score.

If the uploaded artifact is unreadable or inaccessible, rely on the transcript only for Presentation and use `Review (low confidence)`.

## Output expectations

Provide separate evaluations for the **Poster** category and the **Presentation** category. Do not combine them into a single overall grade.

For each category, provide:

- performance band: Exemplary, Proficient, Developing, or Unacceptable;
- suggested numeric score from 0-100;
- concise evidence grounded in the poster or transcript;
- concise concerns, weaknesses, or missing evidence.

For the completed attempt as a whole, provide exactly one review status:
`Review (low confidence)`, `OK (medium confidence)`, or `OK (high confidence)`.

Use the full range of performance bands and scores.
